Can you talk your way past an AI resume screener?

The same résumé, reworded eight ways, run through an open-source AI screener ten times each — to see whether adjectives alone can raise your score without changing a single fact.

AI agents have crept into hiring. Some companies now hand résumés to a model that scores and ranks candidates before a person ever reads them. With a human, the right words can shift their impression of you. So I wanted to know: do the same tricks work on the machine? Can you raise your score by how you write, without touching a single fact?

The setup

I used the system prompt from HiringAgent, an AI résumé screener that was recently open-sourced. The prompt tells the model to act as a technical recruiter and rate a résumé out of 120, split across four categories — open source (35), personal projects (30), work experience (25), technical skills (10) — plus up to 20 bonus points. It carries a lot of baked-in rules: your own GitHub repos don’t count as open source (only contributions to other people’s projects do), a to-do app scores near zero while something complex scores in the 20s, and programs like Google Summer of Code earn bonus points.

I wrote eight versions of one résumé, all stating the same facts:

  • R0 — the plain, honest baseline; a standard résumé.
  • R1–R4 — the same facts with steadily more adjectives per version.
  • RM — a “mirror” that deliberately echoes the screener’s own system prompt, to see if borrowing its terms nets a higher score.
  • RO — an “optimized” version aimed at AI screeners in general, not this one specifically.

Each version went through the reviewer ten times. The agent is an LLM, not a fixed formula, so scores drifted from run to run and between batches. I logged every run.

What I expected

My guess: adjectives would tip the model slightly higher, up to a point. Past some density of flowery language, the model would notice the unnaturalness, start docking points, and read the résumé as worse.

What actually happened

Verbage barely mattered. For most versions the score hardly moved off the baseline — the lightest and densest résumés landed within a point of each other, and I couldn’t confidently say any of them beat R0. Two versions did stand out: the mirror (RM) and R3.

The predicted backlash never came. Piling on adjectives didn’t trigger deductions; it just failed to help. Tracking two mid-scale categories across the density ramp shows the shape of it — one metric wanders upward inside the noise, the other stays flat — but neither moves the way a real improvement would.

The same résumé, two scores

Here’s the part that reframed the whole experiment. When I scored the identical baseline in two separate batches, they came back at 77.1 and 74.9. Same résumé, same prompt, two different numbers. R3 did it too: it scored well, so I reran it — and got an even higher 82.9, up from 81.0. The “wins” were partly just the dice.

My first thought was that the tool was unreliable. But the categorical scores told a subtler story: most categories only moved by a point or so between runs, and stayed reasonably consistent version to version. The prompt was doing its job — it could still tell a weak candidate from a strong one. It just couldn’t resolve fine differences.

The prompt itself explains why. It defines a “low” score as 1–9 points and a “high” score as 20–30. Nothing pins down the gap between a 3 and an 8 — both are just “weak.” So the model wobbles freely inside each band. To detect a real effect from wording, I’d need to measure hundreds, maybe thousands of runs — and from what I saw, the signal would barely clear the baseline anyway. One genuinely strong project is worth 20–30 points; no amount of rewording buys that.

The noise isn’t even

The variation also wasn’t spread evenly. The noisiest judgements were bonus points and open-source credit; the skills list hardly budged. That lines up with the rules — skills are close to a checklist, while “bonus” and what counts as open source are exactly the fuzzy, interpretive calls a language model is least consistent about.

The screener can tell strong from weak. It cannot tell a 3 from an 8 — and that’s exactly the gap wording tries to exploit.

The optimized résumé (RO) was the real surprise: aimed at screeners in general, it didn’t beat the baseline at all — 76.7 against R0’s 77.1. Writing for the abstraction did nothing; only the mirror, written straight at this prompt, nudged ahead, and even that sat inside the noise.

So can you game it?

My experiment was far from conclusive — the sample is small and the target is noisy. But from what I gathered, swapping words and stacking adjectives doesn’t meaningfully change your score. My best average gain was five or six points; one strong project is worth twenty or thirty. Against other factors, three points is nothing.

And yet. Standing next to another candidate, six points can be the whole margin. The effect is real but tiny, and whether tiny matters depends entirely on who you’re being compared to.
